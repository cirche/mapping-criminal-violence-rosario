Research Profile

This project is part of a broader research agenda on organised violence, urban conflict and criminal governance in Latin America.

Academic positioning.

My research is situated at the intersection of organised crime, urban violence and governance in Latin America. This project originates from my fieldwork research in Argentina and has been further developed through my experience as a Research Assistant at the Uppsala Conflict Data Program (UCDP). Focusing on Rosario as a central case, I analyse how criminal actors operate not only as violent organisations, but as embedded structures that regulate territory, shape social relations and interact with state institutions. Rather than treating organised crime as external to the state, my work adopts a relational perspective, examining how legal and illegal orders overlap and co-produce forms of authority in urban contexts. In this sense, the project engages with the concept of crimilegality, understood as the blurred boundary between formal governance and illicit power structures.

Research contribution.

This project is a personal research initiative that builds on my master's thesis and subsequent professional experience. Following my work at UCDP, I developed an original dataset focused on criminal violence in Rosario, adapting event-based methodologies to the study of organised crime in an urban setting. The dataset is structured following UCDP-style coding principles adapted to criminal violence. Using Rosario as an empirical case, the project combines georeferenced event data with qualitative interpretation to examine patterns of violence, territorial control and civilian targeting. The dataset reflects both methodological rigor and contextual knowledge acquired through field research. The approach challenges purely quantitative readings of violence by integrating spatial analysis with context-sensitive interpretation, highlighting how violent events are embedded in local power dynamics. In doing so, the project positions urban criminal violence not as random or purely economic, but as structured, relational and deeply connected to governance processes. More broadly, the project aims to bridge the gap between conflict studies and organised crime research, demonstrating how tools traditionally used to analyse armed conflict can be meaningfully applied to the study of non-state violent actors in Latin American cities.

Methodological approach.

Event-data + qualitative triangulation.
Structured coding of violent events combined with interviews, field insights and documentary analysis.

Spatial analysis of violence.
Georeferenced data to identify territorial patterns, hotspots and dynamics of control within Rosario.

Civilian targeting focus.
Attention to non-combatant victims and coercive practices as indicators of governance and control.

Crimilegal perspective.
Analysis of the overlap between legal and illegal orders in shaping urban authority and violence.

Research interests.

Organised crime and criminal governance

Drug trafficking and transnational networks

Event data and conflict analysis
