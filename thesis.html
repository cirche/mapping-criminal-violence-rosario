Master's thesis · Field research · Argentina

Urban Violence, Organised Crime and Drug Trafficking in Argentina

This thesis forms the empirical and theoretical foundation of the mapping project, connecting fieldwork in Argentina with the later development of an original event-based dataset on Rosario.

Read thesis PDF

Research foundation.

The thesis examines the relationship between urban violence, organised crime and drug trafficking in Argentina, with Rosario as a central case study. It investigates how drug-trafficking networks, criminal gangs, institutional corruption and social vulnerability contribute to the deterioration of urban security and the emergence of hybrid forms of governance.

Research question.

The thesis asks how the expansion of drug trafficking and the infiltration of criminal gangs, together with institutional corruption and social vulnerability, have contributed to the escalation of urban violence and the erosion of security in Argentina.

Key contributions.

Criminal governance and crimilegality.
The thesis analyses how legal and illegal orders overlap in urban contexts marked by weak institutions, inequality and criminal territorial control.

Rosario as a case study.
Rosario is examined as a strategic node of Argentina's drug market and as a site where urban violence, illicit economies and state responses intersect.

Security, defence and militarisation.
The research assesses the evolving role of the Argentine Armed Forces in counter-narcotics policies and the tension between internal security and national defence.

Transnational criminal networks.
The thesis considers the presence of Italian mafias, especially the 'Ndrangheta, in Argentina and their role in broader drug-trafficking circuits.

Methodology.

The thesis adopts an integrated qualitative-quantitative approach, with a strong qualitative emphasis. It draws on fieldwork conducted in Buenos Aires, semi-structured interviews, documentary analysis, official statistics and comparative data. This mixed-methods foundation later informed the development of the original UCDP-style dataset used in this website.

Connection to this project.

The website extends the thesis by translating its core empirical focus into an interactive research tool. The original dataset and map are designed to explore spatial patterns of criminal violence in Rosario and to connect qualitative interpretation with structured event-data methods.
